## Developing your marketing and sales for growth (Reading)

Marketing is the term used to describe the activities which businesses use to generate interest in their products and services, and create a pipeline of prospective customers who intend to buy. Sales are the activities involved in converting an individual or business's intention to buy into an actual purchase. Since sales rely upon a good marketing strategy, and marketing benefits from ongoing customer engagement, the sales and marketing processes intersect. This forms a continuous cycle as known as the marketing and sales cycle.

The cycle begins with the activities in the marketing process: Market segmentation. This first stage in the marketing process is covered in the 10,000 Women course, Fundamentals of Customers and Competition, and involves mapping competitors; assessing business strengths and weaknesses; and dividing broad customer populations into focused customer segments.

### Branding
The branding activity considers the functionality of a product or service, and how it should be priced and packaged. It also involves the communication of company values to customers.

### Product or service development
This activity includes customer-employee interaction and community engagement around the business. It delivers company values through strong customer experience.

### Reach
Reach involves building brand awareness and communicating the Customer Value Proposition (CVP) – a statement which makes explicit the value that a business has to offer – to potential customers.

### Sales
At this fifth stage, the sales process intersects with the marketing process. The sales process focuses on three smaller stages:
1. The lead stage (customers who may make a purchase).
2. The prospects stage (customers who are likely to make a purchase).
3. Customer conversion (customers who make a purchase).

### Customer engagement
In this stage, a business must ensure that the customers who make a purchase are satisfied and receive the appropriate ongoing support that they need.

This stage can also involve ongoing engagement with the brand, for example through social media. Insights obtained during the customer engagement stage can inform ongoing market research and evaluation, which feeds back into the first stage of the cycle.

You should use the cycle to help you to view marketing and sales from strategic perspective. It should encourage you to ensure that your communication to customers is clear and consistent, and to track your marketing and sales activities, and collect data which you can use to drive improvement.

In this course, you will use the marketing and sales cycle as a foundation to develop clear marketing and sales plans for your business.

Before you work through the stages of the marketing and sales cycle of your business, the first step is to identify an overall goal (or goals) and defined measurable objectives that are specific to your business and growth strategy.

## Marketing goals and objectives (Reading)

Clear goals and associated objectives are essential for an effective marketing or sales plan.

### Goals
In a marketing or sales plan, your goal(s) should represent what you want to achieve overall – the destination for your business. A goal may relate to the whole business, respond to a specific challenge, or support one specific growth opportunity.

It is important to remember that goals have to be SMART. It means any goal you set for your business needs to be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant and time-bound. In other words, the goal should be clear, without any vague information, have a quantifiable target or a progress tracking metric to measure the success, directly relevant to your business needs, ambitious yet realistic, and set within a specific timeframe.

Therefore, the goal(s) should reflect your core growth opportunity in alignment with your brand values or company vision.

### Objectives
If a goal is the destination of your business's journey, objectives are the steps along the way. Objectives break down a goal into more manageable and measurable elements, each of which should clearly contribute to achieving the overall goal. Objectives may be short-term compared to their associated goal, relate to several areas of business activity, or separate out different revenue targets.

Consider how objectives contribute to achieving an overall business goal in the following examples:

#### Example 1: Homecare provider for seniors
- Goal: Increase the number of homes served by 5% in the upcoming marketing period or six months, and become the region's most trusted choice for assisted living services. This will enhance the brand's emphasis on integrity and quality of care.
- Objective 1: Launch new care service line for 25 seniors living on their own.
- Objective 2: Secure 30 new clients from current customer referrals.
- Objective 3: Gain 30 clients from competitors.

#### Example 2: Energy management consultancy
- Goal: Increase core consultancy sales revenues by 10% by the end of the calendar year. Build on the brand's reputation for innovation and maintain the business's position as one of the industry's leading energy and technical services firms.
- Objective 1: Expand the compliance consultancy service with 10 small businesses in the local area.
- Objective 2: Increase sales revenue from technical services by 12%.
- Objective 3: Secure five new corporate contracts for sustainability services.

#### Example 3: Craft chocolate makers
- Goal: Increase sales revenues of gift subscriptions in the upcoming quarter by 20%, promoting the business's brand as the top craft chocolatier in the region.
- Objective 1: Increase premium line of gift subscriptions by 10%.
- Objective 2: Increase standard line of gift subscriptions by 3%.
- Objective 3: Launch drinking chocolate subscription at a local department store, with sales of 220 units.

## Branding (Reading)

Now that you have identified an overall goal (or goals) and defined measurable objectives, you are ready to work through the stages of the marketing and sales cycle for your business.

The first stage of the marketing and sales cycle is market segmentation. If you want to explore this specific stage, you can choose to complete one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Customers and Competition. In that course, you can segment your target customers and develop a Customer Value Proposition (CVP) for your target customer segment, highlighting the reasons why those customers should choose to buy your product or service. Remember that while CVP includes a clear and concise explanation of how your product or service is valuable to customers, its uniqueness and strengths in comparison to competition are captured in the unique selling proposition (USP).

One way that your CVP is communicated to potential customers is through branding – this is the second stage of the marketing and sales cycle, and will be explored in this course.

Your brand is more than just a product or service with a memorable logo – it is an external representation of your business and its values. An important foundation for marketing success is making your brand attractive to your target customers, and using it to show how you deliver value and meet their needs.

### Brand values
There are four types of brand value you can communicate to your customers:

- Commercial or functional values focus on practical elements.
- Emotional values appeal to the feelings and well-being of customers.
- Life-changing values help them with personal transformation or skill development.
- Social values reflect contributions to the greater good and give a sense of purpose.

A strong brand will often convey more than one type of value. For example, consumers may not only be interested in brands that offer the best value for money, but also in those with which they can develop an ongoing positive relationship. Occasionally, some brands are able to bring all four types of values in their offering.

An example of this is a meal kit service which is convenient and saves customers time (commercial value) by delivering fresh, pre-portioned ingredients with easy-to-follow recipes. It also offers personalized meals tailored to customer preferences and dietary requirements (emotional value). By promoting healthy eating habits and including nutritionist-approved meals, the business also helps customers achieve their healthy lifestyle needs (life-changing value). Finally, the company donates a portion of its profits to local food banks and charities, allowing customers to contribute to a good cause (social value).

### Using your brand to communicate your values
Your brand is made up of a combination of elements, which create an overall impression of your business and become associated with particular qualities over time (for example, 'durability' or 'sustainability'). These elements include the following:

#### Name
The name of your product, service, or business contains implicit information. Take this example of two different names for the same business type:
- A stationery shop named 'Rodrigues Family Stationers' may give a potential customer the impression of a traditional and reliable brand.
- A stationery shop called 'Stamp!' might suggest that the brand is fun and creative.

#### Digital and physical presence
The website and online presence of your business influence a potential customer's impression of your brand. The same is true of any physical locations, such as shops or offices. Carefully selecting certain fonts or colors can help to reinforce your brand's message. For example:
- Brightly colored or handwritten fonts may give the impression of a bold and creative brand.
- Black text or a formal typeface may suggest that the brand is more professional and conventional.

Other elements, such as staff uniforms, can also help to influence how customers perceive your brand.

#### Personal touch
The tone of voice and the language that you use when describing your products or services can also convey your brand's message.

For example, an informal tone and the use of contractions create a conversational and relaxed message. This could be an appropriate style for a fun and youthful brand.

#### Brand association
Customers may associate your brand with specific images, emotions, or experiences. These associations may be created by your brand's name.

For example, the name 'Mother's Kitchen Soup' could draw on potential customers' positive associations of mothers with affection and caregiving. The kitchen is also often associated with nourishing food and family interactions.

Associations may also be created through the use of graphics and logos. A logo in a bold color and dynamic style of a figure running may create associations of speed and endurance.

### Summary
Great brands apply brand elements intentionally so that they are memorable, compelling, and versatile. They are easy to protect (for example, through the use of a trademark), and easy for customers to share with others.

When deciding how to shape your various brand elements, it is important to have a final goal in mind: driving sales by building an association with your brand.

## Identifying a strong brand (Reading)

Every day, consumers interact with many different brands and some will make a more significant impression than others. A strong brand will help you make a positive first impression and earn customer loyalty.

To guide your brand strategy, consider the perspective of your target customers. There are four factors, each one builds on the previous one:

### 1. Brand awareness
Brand awareness is a combination of brand depth and brand breadth.

Potential customers may be aware of your brand, and will reflect on what they know about it. They will also think about how easily the brand could fulfil their wants or needs. This is known as brand depth.

Customers will also consider when, where, and how often they engage with your brand. This is known as brand breadth, and can support or detract from a brand's messaging. For example, a company that has a clear and compelling Customer Value Proposition (CVP) could devalue that proposition by advertising in newspapers that its target customer segment does not read.

### 2. Brand consideration
Potential customers will then consider what your brand has to offer, building on their initial brand awareness. They often identify the key features of the product or service, and evaluate how your brand's offering differs from your competitors. This is where communicating your Unique Selling Proposition (USP) to the target market will be crucial, as it helps customers understand what sets you apart from competition.

Customers expect good brands to provide most or all of the benefits that competitors offer, which is known as brand parity. A strong brand will also show brand differentiation, and go above and beyond their expectations.

### 3. Brand preference
After reviewing what your brand has to offer, potential customers will decide whether they prefer it to others.

They will consider your unique selling proposition – or brand USP – and its relevance to their needs as a customer. For example, this could be a commitment to quality production, customer service, or good value. A good USP statement simplifies decision-making for customers by highlighting the brand's unique benefits that align with their needs or preferences.

They will also reflect on their brand association, and assess how your brand makes them feel and what particular value it offers them. For example, a brand could reflect their personal ethical values or match their aspirations to be environmentally friendly.

### 4. Brand loyalty
After they have made a purchase, your customers may consider how likely they are to make repeat purchases of your product or service. This is known as brand loyalty.

They may also reflect on how likely they are to recommend your brand to others, which is known as brand advocacy.

## Product/service development (Reading)

You will now consider the importance of product or service development – the third stage of the marketing and sales cycle.

This stage highlights the importance of delivering your brand values through your product or service, to ensure the best customer experience.

## Planning effective marketing (Reading)

You have seen how changing your approach to marketing can impact business growth. Now you will begin exploring the marketing tools and content that you can use to reach your target customers and achieve your marketing objectives. Marketing content is what you say about your product or service, and marketing tools are the ways in which you say it.

### Marketing tools
There are three main categories of marketing tools covered in this course:
1. Long-form tools are used to communicate detailed content to your target customers (for example, articles or blog posts).
2. Short-form tools are used to communicate concise messages to your customers (for example, social media posts or text messages).
3. Conversational tools are used to engage with customers and build a community (for example, starting a conversation with customers who respond to a social media post).

### Marketing content
No matter what tools you select, it is crucial to ensure that your marketing content is carefully planned and developed. Marketing content is information that sends a message about both your brand and Customer Value Proposition (CVP) to your target customer segments.

For your marketing content to be effective, you need to ensure that it is:

#### Relevant
The content should have a customer-centric message, and be appropriate for the core needs and interests of your target customer segment.

#### Engaging
Good content is engaging, and draws in your target customers. Content should enhance your customer's experience rather than interrupt it. For example, potential customers may actively search for articles sharing industry expertise or social media conversations about the latest fashion trends. These can be used to share your marketing message.

#### Consistent
Your marketing activities may require a wide range of different content and blending of various channels and tools – this is sometimes referred to as integrated marketing communications. Therefore, the coordination and alignment of activities like advertising, social media campaigns, sales promotions, and public relations should result in a unified marketing strategy where the content is consistent with your brand message. This requires patience, and ongoing investment to build customer engagement with your brand. In this way, it helps strengthen brand recognition, improve customer understanding, and drive more effective marketing campaigns by aligning all of your messaging and tactics. This consistency builds trust with consumers, increasing the likelihood of customer engagement and conversion.

### Summary
Good content is at the heart of all marketing, and it is particularly important when using digital channels. However, even great content will only be most effective when it is delivered using the right tool.

## Introduction to the sales process (Video)

As a business owner, you know that sales drive growth. However, to build a solid foundation for ongoing sales, it is important to ensure that your processes deliver committed customers who can be champions for your business.

Sales is the fifth stage of the marketing process. In this stage, your market is divided into three groups:
1. Leads who may be interested in buying your product or service.
2. Prospects who are very likely to buy from you.
3. Customers who do buy from you.

These three groups can be visualized in a Sales Funnel.

The Sales Funnel shows the process that leads go through to become customers. As you progress through the sales process, each group becomes progressively smaller. Although your marketing may reach a large number of your target customers, there are a variety of reasons that a large number of these leads never become prospects, and that only a certain proportion of prospects become actual customers.

The primary challenge of the sales process is forming effective relationships with your customers, whether they are individuals or other businesses. These relationships will significantly influence your customers' decision to make a purchase, remain invested in you for the long term, and become advocates for your brand.

The difference between leads and prospects can take time to identify. It is not always easy to distinguish between a customer who might buy from you, and one who is very likely to do so.

Example 1 - B2B software business: Effective marketing brings sales leads to their website. Potential customers can use the website to browse the available products, request more information, or chat live with a customer service representative. Once these potential customers commit to a free trial of a product, they are considered solid prospects. The business's sales team then contacts them to help them learn more about the benefits of the software, and to encourage the organization to sign up for a paid version. In this case, the customer behavior that marks the difference between leads and prospects is commitment to a free trial.

Example 2 - Fashion retail shop: A potential customer browsing the items in the shop can be considered a sales lead. When that person begins to pick up clothing to try on, they have become a solid prospect. At this stage, sales assistants will often engage the potential customer in conversation, and provide any support or advice needed to encourage them to make a purchase. In this case, the behavior that marks the difference between leads and prospects is selecting clothing to try on.

It is important to remember that not all customers will end up following the same customer journey. However, if you identify the most common behaviors or actions that lead to a purchase, it will help ensure that your Sales Funnel remains focused.

If you apply your detailed understanding of your target customers and unique offering to the Sales Funnel, you'll be able to refine the marketing and sales efforts of your business. This will help your staff to convert more leads into customers.

## Strategic planning for your sales activities (Reading)

Strategic planning and coordinated marketing and sales activities are vital when maximizing your business's growth potential. This is the case whether one individual or a large team is responsible for your business's sales activities.

There are three useful considerations that can guide your sales activities:

### 1. Integration with marketing
Marketing and sales are linked in the marketing and sales cycle. Due to this close connection, it is important to ensure that anyone involved in sales and marketing activities has a good understanding of their specific responsibilities. This requires clear and consistent communication around a strategic plan. It can be helpful to do the following:
- Include all relevant team members in your marketing and sales planning.
- Define the behaviors and activities that differentiate a lead from a prospect in your business, and communicate this clearly.
- Invest in a common analytics platform and customer relationship management (CRM) software to encourage efficient use of data.

### 2. Suitability for your business-to-business (B2B) or business-to-consumer (B2C) context
In many cases, the sales approach will be different in B2B and B2C businesses. The B2C purchase decision-making process is likely to involve one or two people, and is often guided by a combination of rational and emotional evaluation. The B2B purchase decision-making process tends to involve multiple stakeholders, and focuses on value drivers such as saving time.

### 3. The structure of your sales team
In some businesses, only one person is involved in sales activities, but others may have a large team. As your business grows, you may consider where you can allocate human resource to maximize the potential of your opportunity. There are a number of different ways that you can structure staff roles to support sales.

#### Generalist sales roles
Generalist sales roles include activities at each stage of the Sales Funnel (for example, engaging with both leads and current customers). In many businesses, this is used to allow a particular member of the sales team to manage individual clients from their being a lead through to being a customer. This can give a strong sense of ownership to each member of the sales team.

However, the skills required to convert cold leads into prospects can be very different from those required to manage existing customers and upsell or cross-sell to them. Therefore, some businesses take a more specialist approach.

#### Specialist sales roles
Specialist sales roles do not retain ownership of a particular client throughout the sale process. Instead, different teams with different skills focus on moving clients from one stage to the next.

Outsourcing some sales activities to external services (for example, engaging an external telesales team) can be very useful for smaller businesses which take a specialist approach.

## Typical features of an analytics platform (Reading)

When considering the use of analytics to support growth, it is important to evaluate how the data will be used to develop your business activities.

There are a range of analytics platforms available which can help you to collect and analyze marketing and sales data. These platforms are used by both small businesses and large corporations. Google Analytics is one of the most popular analytics platforms available, but you should explore the different possibilities to find the most appropriate platform for your business.

### Main features
Analytics platforms are designed to address three main tasks:
1. Data collection and management: Data is collected for a range of standard metrics (such as page views), and the platform can narrow the focus using a range of variables. For example, it is possible to identify the city from which a potential customer views a page.
2. Data analysis and reporting: The analytics platform can generate useful insights about the customer journey through the Sales Funnel. Analysis options include revenue and conversion rates, average order value, and analysis of sales associated with specific marketing campaigns.
3. Data activation: Insights from data collected for previous marketing campaigns can be used to inform future activities. This can allow you to identify the online behaviors and activities that differentiate prospects from leads.

### Using data
Although data collected through analytics generates valuable insights for your business, these are only effective if they are used to improve your approach. If the majority of your sales take place through non-digital channels, you may decide to invest more resources in the collection and analysis of data related to these transactions instead.

Whether the majority of your sales take place online or offline, developing an analytical and data-supported approach to the marketing and sales process will help support ongoing process improvement and growth.
