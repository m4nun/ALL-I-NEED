### The pre-development stage (Video)

Transcript:

Some companies spend a lot of time deciding whether to develop their new product or service. Others starts instantly and later find out that the idea was not viable from the very beginning. The pre-development stage helps you avoid these mistakes.

Product development is strategic with long-term aims. Therefore, before you start, you need to consider these three questions:
1. Does a market exist for your product or service so customers will buy it?
2. Is your product or service technically and commercially viable?
3. How will your product or service improve the efficiency of your company?

First, based on what you and your employees or colleagues already know it is important to make a quick assessment of your ideas potential. Think your way through your whole new product or service idea. Consider each major step from its development until launch. Are there any obvious weaknesses and risks before you even start? Can you spot any difficulties that the business might face at some point in this process?

This brief exercise should not take days but, rather, a few hours, as its purpose is only to convince yourself that your initial decision to commit resources will be justified.

Having completed this quick evaluation, you then need to proceed to a more detailed internal screening with your team. Discuss in detail the potential target market of your new product or service. Can you clearly identify who your customers will be and why? Are you able to see how your new product or service can meet their needs and solve their problems? Try to imagine where and how you will communicate with them about it. It's crucial to check if your market vision is clear or not before you continue.

To proceed with more certainty, you now require a more thorough check on the idea's feasibility, costs and potential income. For this step, it can be useful to work with a colleague, as it will result in better understanding and initiation of the development stage.

If your idea involves the creation of a completely new product, you need to check its feasiblilty first. However, don't ignore this step if it is a modification to an existing product; it can reveal potential risks associated with any extra features you want to introduce.

When it comes to technical feasibility, ask yourself and your colleagues the following question: Are the technologies that the new product or service will use fully developed, evaluated and available? Decide on a brief alternative solution or a plan B and consider your insurance for the worst outcome.

Estimate costs and income at this early stage to help identify resources required and allocate them optimally. Consider key expenses that you are likely to incur during the development process. Then, think of potential margins using a potential realistic product price and volume sold. Compare the two and make a realistic assessment of whether things are looking optimistic.

Finally, draft the timeline of the new product or service development by stage and task. Will it need any reviews at the end of stages? This initial timeline draft often develops into a running document throughout the project.

You now have an outline of the project's viability, target market, potential risks and returns, and an idea of the type of resources you'll need. This will help you proceed or cancel according on the outcome of the screening.

This process of completing in stages will help you alleviate risks associated with newness and prepare you for the next stage of development.

### What can you find out from market research? (Video)

Transcript:

The easiest way for you to lose money in business is to introduce a product or service that customers don't want or need. You need to discover what your customers would like a product to do, that yours or your competitor's products don't already do.

The purpose of market research is to enable informed decision-making. This stage is crucial to ensure that your new product or service is suitable for the target market.

Market research includes the following steps: Planning, which includes specific research problems or objectives, Designing research with methods selected and sampling, Collecting data, Analyzing data, Communicating the results of this analysis to the decision-maker. It uses multiple sources of information, both internal and external, already available and yet to be gathered.

There are two types of market research often conducted by companies, depending on their purpose.

The first type is applied or problem-solving research, which addresses a specific pragmatic problem, such as: needing a better understanding of the marketplace, finding out why a strategy or tactic failed, improving pricing or segmentation strategies, such as organizing your customers based on demographic, geographic, behavioral, and psychographic factors.

The second type is basic or problem-identifying research, which explores market knowledge overall rather than focusing on a particular issue. For instance, market potential, market awareness of the product or service and the brand, characteristics research, looking at trends and forecasting.

In many cases, decision-makers prefer to use the findings of both types, where possible, and may carry out research themselves or use external companies.

There are typically different sources available to access this information. These are also known as 'secondary and primary research'.

When conducting market research, it is always easier and much more efficient to start with secondary research, which is information that has already been gathered. This can mean working with data and information internally available within your business or available externally, that has been collected for some other purpose but can still be very applicable.

In turn, primary research is gathering new information. It involves the design and selection of specific methods to collect and analyze fresh data to help address very specific objectives. This process can involve use of focus groups, customer feedback surveys, shopping behavior observations, and individual post-service interviews.

The main advantage of primary research lies in the ability to ask your potential and existing customers questions about your products and services.

So, how should you approach market research?

Start with the secondary research of available data that can help you identify potential customers, competition in the market, partners and collaborators. It can also highlight key ways in which the market operates when it comes to pricing, promotion, consumption and distribution.

Then, continue with primary research if needed, which you can tailor to specific objectives of finding out what customers are looking for in new products or services, why current ones don't work, and what unsatisfied needs customers still have. You could also focus on market potential and awareness in general, or ask for customer feedback on a specific prototype or trial.

When executed rigorously, market research can minimize uncertainty around new product or service development, and provide clear direction on how to move forward.

### Using existing market and industry information (Reading)

You now know what typical market research involves. In business, it's important to note that market research projects can vary dramatically depending on the following factors: the type of new product or service, the resources which are available to you, the market research methods you use and how you apply them.

Getting started: using secondary market research

Once you have set your market research objectives, you should always start your initial market research with secondary research, which is working with data that already exists.

Secondary data have been collected for some purpose other than for your own market research, either by others, or yourself in the past. For example, this may include reports and studies by government agencies, trade associations or other businesses within your industry. It could also include the results from past surveys of your customers, or your sales reports. This data is very useful and informative for the progress of any market research, such as identifying potential customers and competition in the market.

Sources of secondary data

There are two main sources of secondary data in market research:

1. Internal secondary data

This is all the data available within your business. They are typically generated by the business as part of the ongoing business process. Examples of internal data sources include:
- company accounts (customer account details, advertising expenditure, sales-force expenses, inventory data etc.)
- customer databases (all customer data, including names, contact details, purchase history etc.)
- sales reports (sales people, their territories/areas, sales performance details etc.)

All of these can be useful starting points in many research projects. For example: looking at customer purchase history can help with the understanding of what they typically value and need, so that you can reshape your ideas to suit the audience.

2. External secondary data

These are facts and figures relating to the industry, market, competition, and the country where your business operates. Some key examples of external secondary data are:
- external databases (commercially available customer lead databases, global data on country economic indicators, risk assessments etc.)
- directories and published sources (market reports, industry reports, including trends and forecasts)
- trade association reports in your industry sector
- syndicated services (a service that provides overall market insights to businesses by collecting and selling pools of data).

Advantages and disadvantages of using secondary data

Advantages:
- Allows you to clarify and refine your research objectives.
- Usually provides a solution to the problem.
- Provides alternatives for primary research methods.
- Helps identify potential problems or difficulties.
- Provides necessary background information for the research project.

Disadvantages:
- There may be a lack of availability.
- The data that is available may not be fully relevant.
- The data has been gathered by others so it may be inaccurate.
- There may not be enough data for your needs.

Tips for evaluating secondary data

Follow these tips when evaluating secondary data:
- Cost: to reduce your costs, consider accessing freely-available data first and only pay for data if absolutely necessary.
- Time: it is crucial to devote a significant amount of time to any available market and industry reports. This allows you to properly refine and develop your new product or service to meet the market requirements.
- Consider the reliability and credibility of the data based on its source and ask yourself the following questions:
  - How up to date is it?
  - Have you explored the specific factors and their relevance to you?
  - What methods have been used to collect or analyze the data?
  - How many respondents have been included in the data collection?
  - Are there any errors and any other relevant criteria?

### Conducting your specialist research (Reading)

You are now familiar with how secondary research can help in the development process. You are now ready to learn how primary research can bring even more clarity and provide answers to some of your most challenging questions.

Primary research

Primary research involves working with new data collected for your specific market research purpose. In contrast to secondary research, this data is often harder to obtain due to the need to dedicate more resources and time to it. However, primary data is the key to understanding customer needs and learning about possible ways to address them. Leveraging secondary research methods first helps to refine your approach before beginning primary research.

You can conduct primary research by following two distinctive paths, qualitative and quantitative:

1. Qualitative research

Qualitative research allows you to ask the question: 'Why?'

It aims to gain a deeper understanding of customers' motivations and actions. Typically, it has no predefined answers and provides rich textual information on a topic. For instance, you might consider obtaining customer feedback on potential upgrades to an existing product. You could ask for their preferences on usability and the key features they value in your products. You could ask your potential customers to share their thoughts on needs and problems that are hard to address, and which no competitors have met so far.

2. Quantitative research

Quantitative research deals with quantifiable, numerical data and its analysis which enables generalizations about patterns and trends across a wider group. Quantitative research is particularly useful when you have already acquired insights and details via qualitative research and you have refined your objectives. A well-designed questionnaire can then provide the remaining answers you need to progress in the new product or service development process.

How can these methods be used?

Qualitative research: Focus groups

Focus groups are one of the most popular qualitative methods in market research, specifically when it comes to new product development or testing. Focus groups are interviews conducted by a moderator among a small group of respondents in an unstructured and natural manner.

Focus groups can provide you with a rich understanding of your customer's requirements across all steps in the development cycle.
- Concept-testing: Focus groups allow you to examine customer reactions and feedback on your concept product or service.
- Price impressions: Focus groups allow you to find out what your potential customers would be willing to pay for your new product or service.
- Advertising: Focus groups are an insightful way of finding out how creative concepts might or might not work among the target audience.
- Audience receptions: Focus groups can be used to gain early reactions on already-launched products or services.

Quantitative research: Surveys

Surveys are used to obtain comparable information about a topic such as your new product or service. Surveys are typically designed as a structured questionnaire which includes multiple-choice questions. Often respondents are asked to rate how feel about certain features on a scale of interest (such as from 1-5).

Surveys allow you to obtain consistent information from large groups, as all respondents answer the same questions. Surveys are useful when information required from respondents is not sensitive or complex so they can answer the closed questions easily. Structure your survey in the following sequence:
- Introductory questions that make the respondents aware of what the survey will be about.
- A core section with the most important questions such as: a) The importance of different product features and functions, and b) Perceptions of values and prices.
- A set of demographic questions to help you understand your target market characteristics and to build customer profiles.

By structuring your survey in this sequence, it allows you to set the scene for the respondent by easing them into the survey with some introductory questions. It then keeps them focused on the core questions and finishes with demographic questions.

Combining approaches

Focus groups and surveys often work well in combination when researching the market prior to creating your new product or service. Insights that you get from talking to potential customers can then be tested in a larger sample of respondents to predict customer reactions and perceptions after launch.

### Finding your sample group (Reading)

Once you have made your decision on which market research activities and methods to use, you then need to think about a sampling process. Sampling involves studying a representative proportion of the target population, i.e. you look at a smaller group that has similar characteristics to your target audience. This technique saves time, resources and may even be more accurate.

Selecting your sample group

Who would be your best respondents? Respondents need to be able to answer the questions in your questionnaire and their views need to matter to your business.

One of the easiest ways to approach sampling is to define your target audience and then estimate the sample size needed. To do this, consider writing down essential criteria of the respondents necessary for your research like this example:

Imagine you are conducting market research with an aim of introducing a new version of a hand cream for mature skin in the UK. You decide to survey people aged 55 and above living in the UK who have used the previous version of this hand cream in the past six months. These people are your existing customers because they have used the previous version of your hand cream.

Generally, a sufficient sample size would be between 200 and 1000 respondents depending on the new product or service and the time and resources available.

Approaching your sample group

Finally, ask your respondents to complete your specially designed questionnaire by utilizing your personal and business connections and social media to increase your reach. Usually, a combination of these networks works well and provides access to a broader selection of customers.

You might also consider third-party paid services, especially when the product you are developing is complex in its nature or radically new.

### Testing your product or service (Reading)

This stage helps you to optimize the new product or service you are developing.

You have now conducted your market research and clarified your new product or service idea even more. Have the previous steps led you to adapt your development plan or reshape your thinking?

The next step in the development process is concept-testing. This involves some creativity and constructive criticism from you and your team. It means that you can then be confident to proceed further with an even more evolved new product or service development plan.

What is the concept-testing stage?

The concept-testing stage comprises generating and testing concepts for their functionality and feasibility, with customer needs in mind. This stage helps you to optimize the new product or service you are developing.

You may already have a feeling for what a good solution could be when it comes to your new product or service. You might believe generating more options will waste important time that could be invested into the development process. However, single solutions are rarely recipes for success. Designing and testing multiple different concepts is much more likely to bring you to the best possible solution.

The purpose of concept-testing is to generate new and better ways of doing things. There are several actions you can take to make this stage as effective as possible:
- List the customers' performance requirements for your product or service, and see how each requirement can be achieved by different means.
- Generate multiple concepts as this will enable you to better evaluate them later.
- Treat concepts as solutions to customer problems. See them as the 'how' (your product or service) to the 'why' (customer need).
- Separate the process of generating concepts from criticizing them, as this may damage some potentially successful concepts in the early stages of their creation.

Concept-testing is an important step in any new product or service development journey because it can help reveal issues or mistakes. These issues or mistakes may have been overlooked before due to human error, insufficient market research, or supply chain problems.
