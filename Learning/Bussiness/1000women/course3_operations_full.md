Understanding the importance of operational processes
Loading audio...
The production and delivery of products or services are carried out by operational processes. These operational processes transform raw inputs into desired products or services, by means of capital equipment, technology, and human resources.
A process can be defined as a group of activities that:
take a set of inputs
synthesize and add value to them 
produce an output.
This is demonstrated in the diagram below, which illustrates the input-process-output model. 
The benefits of good operational processes
Good operational processes can provide significant value to your business in three areas:
1. Improve efficiency
      A well-designed and executed process can improve delivery timescales,      enhance employee productivity, and reduce production costs.
2. Add value
      Efficient processes can provide value for your target customers      through faster deliveries, streamlined customer experience during the      purchase process, and lower prices.
3. Provide competitive advantage
      Processes can provide your business with competitive advantage linked      to product quality, delivery time, and price.
Evolving business processes as you grow
Owners of growing businesses often find that processes which work well on a smaller scale need to evolve as staff, customers, and turnover increase. 
For example, the owner setting up a small business may decide that they will offer a personal service for each customer, such as a style consultation provided by a boutique fashion retailer. When the business first opens, there may be no formally defined process for this service as the business owner delivers it directly herself. However, as the business grows, this service may need to be delivered by different people in the business. To ensure that customers get a consistent quality of service, the process must be formally defined.
Many operational processes also involve technology, which can change rapidly, either through general progress or in response to changing circumstances. You may need to change or update your processes as these changes happen. 
Your business processes can also reinforce your target customers’ impressions of your brand. This can improve the effectiveness of your marketing communications, and increase sales and customer loyalty. To find out more about enhancing your competitive advantage or communicating your brand values to your target customers, you can choose to complete one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Customers and Competition and Fundamentals of Sales and Marketing.
In the example above, the business owner may want to reinforce their brand messaging, which suggests that the boutique offers quality products and service that customers will value. They decide that as part of the style consultation service offered at their stores, assistants will offer to take photographs that customers can share with their friends before or after making a purchase. This process adjustment may reinforce the business’s brand values of high quality, personalized customer service.
In the remainder of this lesson, you will begin to evaluate your current business processes, and consider whether they are a strong foundation to support sustainable growth.

## Assessing your business processes

Assessing your business processes
Transcript
​
​To understand a process, you need to look in detail at every step. ​Each stage of the process may seem obvious to you. ​However, by taking the time to assess each step in detail, ​you'll be able to identify areas where ​your processes are not as efficient as they could be. ​You can then evaluate how to get the most out of your business. ​Let's consider an example of a process. ​These are the steps a client must take to hire a lawyer from this example law firm. ​1 – The prospective client calls the main contact number for the firm. 
​2 – A receptionist records basic contact information from the client, ​and passes this on to the first available account support officer. ​3 – The account support officer contacts ​the potential client for further information about their needs. ​4 – The support officer then ​passes this information to the relevant department within the business, ​for example, commercial law or family law. ​5 – After a department has been assigned, ​support staff provide an overview of appropriate services. ​6 – When a service is agreed, they confirm the terms and price of the service. ​7 – The support staff then sends ​the client a contract to confirm the hiring of the lawyer. ​When a process is broken down into its component steps, ​it becomes easier to identify which steps require improvement, ​and which ones are essential as part of the service delivery. 
​One way to approach this is to assign each step one of three categories: ​1 – Value-Added steps. ​2 – Non-Value-Added steps. ​3 – Necessary Non-Value-Added steps. ​Value-Added steps enhance your product or service by meeting a customer need. ​By investing your resources in these steps, ​you can make your offer more attractive to customers. ​In the example, the overview of ​appropriate services offered by the law firm adds value for the customer, ​enabling them to make an informed decision. ​You may also find Non-Value-Added steps in your processes. 
​These steps drain business resources and ​time while providing no value to the product or service. ​When you map your process, ​it may highlight stages that could be made more efficient with small adjustments. ​In this example, the prospective client has to wait for ​an account support officer to be assigned before being able to provide ​detailed information about their needs. This ​wastes time and adds no value to the service of the firm. ​By training the firm's receptionists to assign the client to a department, ​this waiting time could be significantly reduced. ​Although some steps do not add value for your customers, ​they are still crucial to the process. These are ​called necessary Non-Value-Added steps. 
​It is often impossible to remove these steps, ​but you may want to consider how to reduce the resources that you ​invest in them, to maximize your operational efficiency. ​In the law firm example, ​collecting information from the prospective client does ​not add value but is necessary to the process. ​To maximize efficiency, the firm could ensure that ​its receptionists have a standardized list of information to collect, ​saving time later in the process. ​Next, you will explore in more detail ​how to reduce inefficiency in your business processes.

## Identifying waste in your processes
**Type:** reading
**URL:** https://www.coursera.org/learn/10k-women-7/supplement/xuOht/identifying-waste-in-your-processes

Loading audio…
Identifying waste in your processes
Any step of a process which is not adding value is waste. No business can completely eliminate waste from its processes, but identifying waste is the first step of the journey towards greater efficiency. Understanding the type of waste in your business processes will enable you to identify the most appropriate actions to reduce it.
Waste can be grouped into seven main categories:
1. Waiting
Waiting occurs when goods are not moving, or a step in the execution of a service is being held up. You may notice that much of a product’s lead time (the time taken to produce the product) is tied up in waiting for the next stage of production. This could be due to the following:
A raw material storage plan that is not based on the production flow, which causes constant movement of material across the manufacturing area.
An inefficient workflow, which creates unnecessary bottlenecks between process stages.
An administrative step in the process that requires frequent updates of files in the middle of a process.
Waiting can lead to increased costs and reduced productivity. Aligning processes so that one feeds directly into the next can reduce waiting times.
2. Transporting
Transporting products or people between steps does not add any value to the product. Excessive movement and handling can also impact product quality.
This is often thought of as a physical process, even in a service-based company where paperwork is moved around the office. However, transporting can also include electronic movement, and the waste associated with sending files and documents unnecessarily or repeatedly.
3. Overproduction
Overproduction is the creation of more products than is required. Not only does this increase your inventory, it can also lead to lower quality and productivity, higher lead times, and higher storage costs. A design agency that creates more designs than agreed for a particular contract is an example of overproduction in a service business.
One solution to this problem is to schedule and produce only what can be sold within a defined planning period (for example, a week), with a small contingency margin.
4. Inappropriate processing
You may be using expensive, high-precision equipment when simpler options would be sufficient. This overproduction can increase costs and production time. One solution to reduce this waste is investing in smaller, more flexible equipment whenever possible. 
For service businesses, not just fulfilling a client contract, but going above and beyond it is an example of inappropriate processing. This could extend to the point where the quality produced for the client far exceeds the resources allocated to the project, and what the client actually needs.
5. Unnecessary inventory
This is similar to overproduction, and involves keeping more items in stock than are immediately required. This can cause increased lead times, use up productive and expensive resources, and affect your cash flow, which you can explore in the 10,000 Women course, Fundamentals of Financial Planning. Increasingly, this also includes digital storage, particularly data that needs to be managed.
6. Unnecessary motion
Unnecessary motion occurs when people do not have the resources they require to fully complete their tasks at their station. This may include having to fetch tools, data sheets, or materials from other areas of the workplace. This is often frustrating for your staff and represents wasted time. 
For service-based businesses, this might be time wasted looking for records, moving around or between offices, or searching through digital data and files. You may want to analyze job roles with excessive motion, and investigate using technology or other means to redesign or improve them in order to minimize waste.
7. Defects 
Defects in manufacturing often have a direct impact on the revenue and profit of a business. Product defects lead to rework, re-inspection, rescheduling, delays, and scrap – all of which add significant cost.
Similarly, the costs of having a project returned from an unsatisfied client because it fails to meet the brief or their expectations can be significant. This can also be damaging to your business’s reputation. This kind of waste includes the need to revisit or rework stages of a service before delivery.
Employee engagement and continuous improvement initiatives are useful strategies to reduce defects in businesses.
If you evaluate the steps in your business processes carefully, you will be able to identify ways to maximize value, reduce waste, and improve efficiency as your business grows.

## Creating competitive advantage with processes
**Type:** reading
**URL:** https://www.coursera.org/learn/10k-women-7/supplement/fDFZJ/creating-competitive-advantage-with-processes

Creating competitive advantage with processes
Loading audio...
Improving efficiency within your business adds value for customers. If you make further enhancements, you provide a competitive advantage, and give your customers a compelling reason to buy your offering over that of your competition. If you wish, you can explore competitive advantage in more depth in one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Customers and Competition. 
As you review the example below, consider how your own business processes could offer a competitive advantage.
Example: Dara's department store
In a fiercely competitive market, Dara's department store is differentiated by offering free home delivery for purchases above a certain amount. Previously, it partnered with a small delivery chain so orders could be delivered within one day. Many competitors soon copied this by offering home delivery services, eliminating the department store’s competitive advantage.  
How can the business enhance its process to gain back its competitive advantage?
Dara looked at an analysis of the department store's customers and competition, identifying how her business could differentiate further.
She realized that her customers’ expectations of delivery had increased. Now that competitors also offered free home delivery, customers seemed to want a more efficient service with faster delivery times. Although one-day delivery used to satisfy them, they now wanted same-day delivery options.
This was an insight Dara could capitalize on. Her business partnered with a local taxi service, which enabled delivery of customer orders within hourly time slots on the same day. This created a new competitive advantage for the business.
It is often possible to solve customer pain points or meet new customer needs by modifying processes to deliver value that is not currently offered in the market.

## Developing a business process to support growth
**Type:** reading
**URL:** https://www.coursera.org/learn/10k-women-7/supplement/Ku75s/developing-a-business-process-to-support-growth

Developing a business process to support growth
Loading audio…
Now that you know which processes to improve in your business, it is time to consider the best ways to do this.
As a business owner, you know that the customer is at the heart of everything you do. A deep understanding of customers is important to ensure that your process improvements meet their needs, and support business growth. 
It is also important to gather data within your business, to ensure that the approach you select is a good fit. Data gathering is the first step to measure the current performance of your processes. Once you have data, you can explore how to improve performance and develop greater efficiency.
Example: Sara
In Lesson 2, you explored the fault reporting process in Sara’s B2B internet services business.
The process has multiple pathways, depending on the complexity of the fault that the customer needs to report. There are a number of stages where the customer journey may be overly extended. This results in more time spent checking emails and completing administrative tasks, as well as technical ones. Sara thinks that improving the efficiency of this process will be better for her customers. It would also help to attract new customers, if the customer experience is better than that provided by her competitors.
To improve the efficiency of this process, Sara could explore different methods to reduce the time customers spend in the reporting process. This might mean investing in automated systems that inform customers of the progress of their fault resolution, or information systems to update staff on the priority of all reported faults.
Both of the options may be costly, but could have a big impact on the business.
How to make the decision: use externally sourced data
Sara surveys her customers, to become better informed about what they need from her service before making the investment. They say that the wait times are not a big problem, but that they would like to be better informed about the progress made when they are waiting for a fault to be fixed. Due to this feedback, Sara decides that she will invest in integrating an online tracking system for her business.
This not only helps her current customers feel listened to and involved, but will also provide a way for her to stand out from her competitors when she is trying to attract new customers. Because it is responding to a customer need, this gives Sara a focus advantage. It will also help to reinforce her brand values, which promote clear communication and efficient customer service. 
Example: Marta
In Lesson 2, you also explored the cake production process in Marta’s bakery.
Quality is very important in this business, as Marta’s customers have high expectations of her cakes. However, as she is the only member of staff conducting quality checks, this creates a significant amount of wait time and adds additional cost to the production of each cake.
This is a common situation for business owners who have specialized knowledge and retain key operational tasks. To improve the efficiency of this process, Marta could design and document a systematic quality check system. She could then identify staff, ideally those who are working on that stage of the process themselves, and train them thoroughly to complete the quality checks. After this, Marta could hand the process over to her staff and monitor its success. Alternatively, Marta could make a new hire and find a quality assessor from outside of her current business.
Marta thinks that training her staff would be better, as it would be less costly and less disruptive to the business. However, she wants to make sure that the quality of her cakes (which the customers love) is maintained.
How to make the decision: use internally sourced data
Marta decides to do the following:
Speak to her staff, to see whether they are interested in being trained in quality assessment.
Review their personal development files, to identify suitable staff for training.
Check her budgets, to evaluate whether it would be more costly to train her staff or hire someone new.
Marta’s staff were very enthusiastic about training in quality assessment, so Marta decided to take that approach when developing her process.
By implementing these changes, Marta removes the bottleneck in her process. She also now saves money without reducing the quality of her cakes. This allows her to pass on the savings to her customers, giving her a cost advantage and strengthening her brand values of quality at a mid-range price point. The approach also frees up her time to focus on growth activities, and provides her team members with valuable professional development.

Review your operational process with your team (Exercise 4)
It is now time to review your own processes, and identify how you can enhance competitive advantage in your business. You can introduce this as a team activity, using the operational process maps that you completed in Lesson 2.
This session should be carefully planned to ensure that you have a productive meeting that results in useful insights and encourages your staff to take ownership of the processes as they develop. Before you begin, think about the internal and external data that you will need to help you make decisions. For example, if you want to conduct customer surveys, you should do this in advance and bring the results to the meeting.
It is important to consider the following factors when planning your operational process review:
Facilitator
Team
Environment
Scope of the review
Objectives and outcomes
Facilitator
An engaged and supportive facilitator will help to ensure that your review of the operational processes is productive and focused. They should support staff to think critically about the process, and challenge assumptions to ensure that the review is rigorous. An objective facilitator with an energetic approach underpins the success of a process review. It may be more appropriate to find an external facilitator for your meeting than to take on this role yourself.
Team
For your review to be meaningful, it should involve staff who are directly involved in the process (for example, frontline staff responsible for delivering your service to customers). Gathering the feedback and insights of these stakeholders will help ensure that you can address the limitations and exploit the potential of the process currently followed in your business. It will also ensure that you consider the impact of any changes on the staff who will be implementing them.
Environment
The place where you hold your review will set the tone for the meeting. If you provide large copies of the process maps and materials that staff can use to record their thoughts and ideas (for example, whiteboards or sticky notes), it will encourage active participation from those involved and set a collaborative, purposeful tone. Alternatively, if you wish to hold the session online, ensure that you distribute any resources you can in advance, and then use shared documents or shared task lists to work collaboratively.     
Scope of the review
It is important to define the scope of the process review in advance, to set and manage staff expectations. This may involve explaining what can and cannot be done as part of the review. For example, you may need to consider the budget, and be clear about how much money will be available to make any changes. You should also set a clear timeframe for the process improvement.
Objectives and outcomes
Before the meeting, you should share the objectives and expected outcomes of the review. This is particularly important as your staff will need to consider the process changes from the customer's point of view, and understand how they contribute to competitive advantage (which they may not normally think about). 
If you share the objectives and expected outcomes in advance, you can ensure that everyone involved has a clear understanding of the requirements of the review, and is aware of the steps that will follow it. For example, you may identify specific processes in your business that you want to better align with your brand values, to improve the effectiveness of your marketing. By asking team members to consider this in advance, you will be able to benefit from a more in-depth discussion and idea refinement in the review meeting. 
A successful process review is the result of careful planning, engaged participation, and clearly defined criteria for success. Invest the time and resource to prepare effectively, and consider these key factors. This will enable you to evaluate and innovate more meaningfully, and support operational efficiency.
Once you have reviewed the operational processes, it is important to measure the impact of your changes to ensure that you are seeing the benefits in terms of business growth.

## Selecting appropriate improvement targets
**Type:** reading
**URL:** https://www.coursera.org/learn/10k-women-7/supplement/4fpOn/selecting-appropriate-improvement-targets

Selecting appropriate improvement targets
Loading audio...
As you saw in the video, it is important to select both lead and lag measures to enable you to effectively evaluate the impact of your improvement changes. 
Lead measures (activity measures) have several benefits:
They help you when things are going well, and indicate when preventative action may be necessary.
They help your business respond proactively to problems, rather than wait for the results.
They can improve the reaction time to internal or external changes, and make the business more efficient and growth-oriented.
Lag measures (outcome measures) can offer further benefits:
They provide a good picture of the health of your business. 
They help balance supply and demand.
They optimize your resources. 
Setting appropriate targets for different business processes is a critical leadership activity. One commonly used approach to guide target-setting is to ensure that each target is SMART. SMART stands for:
Specific
Targets should clearly describe the required actions and outcomes. Specific targets help to ensure that your business is focusing on the most relevant areas, and avoids targets that are too vague to measure accurately.
Measurable
Targets must be measurable, and indicators of progress and success should be defined. These indicators can relate to both quality and quantity. The targets you set for your business should be measurable with the current resources and systems that you have. 
Attainable
Targets must be achievable in the required time frame, using the resources available to you. Identifying realistic attainable targets can be a challenge. A useful strategy is to involve your team in the process of setting targets. This ensures that they take ownership of those that are linked to their role, and that they are practical and feasible.
Relevant
Targets should be appropriate for your business, and relate clearly to your plans for growth. Most process improvement targets should address a business growth goal or customer need, as they are the means to an end: one of your business targets.
Timely
Targets should have a defined time frame. Ideally, you will also be able to set target milestones with clearly defined expectations for the progress required at those points. Process improvement does not happen immediately, and it is important to measure frequently to help you track progress. A combination of lead and lag measures can help ensure that you track the rate of process improvement.    
Summary
One of the most challenging aspects is ensuring that your goals are measurable, as this relies on you having good quality data that you access easily and cost effectively. You also need to have confidence in its reliability over time. This is often a challenge for small businesses in particular. In the next discussion forum, you will be able to share some practical experience about how you seek to overcome this.
