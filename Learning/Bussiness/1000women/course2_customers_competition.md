## 4. Your Future Customers and Markets (Reading)

There are many ways to gather useful information about your customers. These include the following:

- Reviewing existing research reports about your sector. This includes industry-level information and may include information about customers.
- Exploring purchase history and loyalty program data. This can help you understand buying habits, spending patterns and product preferences.
- Collecting data to find out more about your customers through post-purchase surveys and customer feedback/reviews.
- Checking what your customers post about your products online, including on external review websites and social media.
- Performing website analytics, utilising customer relationship management (CRM) software and tracking email campaign metrics. These processes can be significantly simplified with the use of AI for data analysis, customer information organisation and customer behaviour pattern generation.
- Holding focus groups and/or interviews for more in-depth understanding of customer decision-making.
- Surveying your customers in more detail (in-house or through a market research agency).

Business owners will often split their customers into three simple categories: current customers, potential customers, and unforeseen customers.

### 1. Current customers
These are customers who have previously bought your product or service. You may already have some information about them, such as:
- the product or service they purchased from you in the past
- their reasons for making the purchase
- the purchase features they valued the most.

To ensure that you continue to meet your current customers' needs, you need to further develop your understanding of them. If customers are unhappy with a product or service, it is easier than ever before for them to switch to a new provider, or try a different product. This can be a challenge for those trying to retain their current customers.

It is important to think about how your current customers will respond to your new business opportunity. Will they all be interested in buying your new offering? You may need to consider how to communicate its value to ensure that current customers are not alienated by the change.

If your business is facing changes in the market or sector, you may need to consider how to best keep your existing customers.

### 2. Potential customers
Potential customers are interested in what you have to offer (or they could be interested, if it is appropriately communicated), but have not yet made a purchase. You may have targeted them in the past, but as you expand or change your offering, the demographic of this group may also change.

If you understand the factors that these potential customers value most, it will help you adjust your offering (or the way that you communicate it) to successfully convert them into customers. Although current customers ensure the stability of your business, new customers are your engine for growth.

### 3. Unforeseen customers
Unforeseen customers are potential buyers who have not previously purchased your product or service but suddenly become customers. This may be because they have found an innovative use for your product or service that you did not anticipate. For example, you may be selling clothing that targets gym-going customers, but find that people who buy your products also use it as loungewear. This could open a whole new segment of customers for your business.

It may not always be easy to identify the potential of unexpected customers. Sometimes it might just be a one-off purchase by an individual customer, while at other times it might be indicative of a larger opportunity to attract a new group of buyers. As you grow, you should remain open to innovative uses of your product or service, and consider broad awareness among non-target groups, to encourage unexpected customers for your offering.

### Customer Personas
Now that you have considered current, potential and unforeseen customers, think about who your ideal customers are, as this can help you improve your offering. It is common to feel that your offering is for everyone, but being this general can limit your marketing's impact. The 'Customer persona' approach is a detailed description of your ideal customer based on a fictional character. Try it below and make some notes:

- Define demographics: write down the age, gender, occupation, and other relevant characteristics of your ideal customer.
- Identify their goals and challenges: what they want to achieve and what stops them.
- Understand behaviors and preferences: imagine what do they do in their free time? How do they prefer to communicate?
- Create a short profile of a full persona by combining all the above details.

Example: "Meet Lou, a 42-year-old flower boutique owner. She wants to increase sales but finds social media marketing overwhelming. Lou enjoys listening to podcasts and prefers quick, concise email updates."

This exercise can give you a clear picture of who your customers are and might be, as well as how to connect with them effectively.

If you develop a deep understanding of your current and future customers, and the factors they value most, you will be able to ensure that the way you communicate your offering focuses on their needs. This will help you gain and retain customers, and ensure the success of your new growth opportunity.

## 5. The Benefits of Segmenting Your Customers (Reading)

To get to know your customers better, and identify opportunities to increase your business's growth, you can use customer segmentation. Customer segmentation divides customers into different categories based on their characteristics and requirements.

Segmenting your customers allows you to focus your offering on a narrower target market. This makes it easier to refine your product or service, and ensure that you are providing value that will help attract and retain customers to support business growth. Overall, it will support your marketing, research and development, production and delivery, and pricing activities.

### Marketing
Customer segmentation supports targeted marketing. For example, many social media sites offer a variety of ways to segment your audience using multiple variables. These include core data, such as geography and demographics, as well as other, more specific variables that allow you to target groups such as loyal customers, customers on mobile devices, and people who are similar to your current customers.

If you want to explore your market, competitors and target customers further, and develop clear marketing and sales plans for your business, you can complete one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Sales and Marketing.

### Research and development
Research and development in a business can also be influenced by customer segmentation. You will need to consider the core needs of your target customers when you work on developing your offering or launch a new product or service.

### Production and delivery
In some cases, segmentation will also impact production and delivery processes. For example, if you segment your market by geography and decide to expand into new areas, your delivery processes and associated costs may change.

### Pricing
The customer segments you identify can influence your pricing and costing structure. Some customer segments are less price-sensitive than others, and will be happy to pay more for a greater-value offer. You may be able to charge a premium price for your products or services if you understand what value matters most to this group of customers.

As you continue to develop your opportunity, remember to focus on your target customer segments. You will be more likely to succeed in retaining and acquiring new customers if you can develop your offering to appeal to them and meet their needs.

## 6. Customer Segmentation (Video)

As a business owner, it is important to remember that not all customers are the same. They often have different needs and value different things in the products and services that they use. Some businesses have one very specific kind of customer, but many have a diverse customer base with a range of motivations. Those customer types can have different characteristics and requirements, and sometimes these are contradictory. It is very challenging for any product or service to meet all the needs of their customers. Segmenting your customers by grouping them in different ways can be a powerful way to identify your target markets, and can help you to differentiate yourself from the competition. Segmentation requires you to identify key characteristics that your customers share (for example, their likely age, location, or shared needs such as a preference for convenience over price), and then to consider the market for each of these variables in turn.

This then can help you to understand where you are most likely to attract further customers. There is a range of different variables that you can use to segment your customer base. Some of the key categories of segmentation variables include: demography, geography, volume, psychographics, behaviors.

- **Demographic segmentation** groups customers based on their age, gender, or income. For example, customers might be predominantly women or fall into a typical age range of 40 to 50 years old.
- **Geographic segmentation** involves grouping customers based on their location. This could be based on a wide geographic range (for example, by country) or a narrow one (for example, areas of a town or city).
- **Volume segmentation** groups customers based on how much of a particular product they buy on each purchase occasion. For example, customers who regularly buy grocery staples in bulk, or those who only purchase luxury products occasionally.
- **Psychographic segmentation** involves grouping customers based on their lifestyles and personalities. For example, customers who place a high value on maintaining a healthy lifestyle, or customers with limited time but considerable disposable income.
- **Behavioral segmentation** groups customers based on their attitudes and social status. For example, green consumers who place significant value on environmentally friendly products and services that match their ethical priorities.

How you choose to segment your potential customer base will depend on your product or service, and on your competitive advantage.

## 8. The Customer Buying Process (Reading)

Now you have segmented your market, let's look at how the Customer Buying Process will vary for different customer segments.

The Customer Buying Process (CBP) describes the decision-making actions your customers take before they purchase your product or service. Although there may be a generic process for your product or service, different customer segments are likely to have different priorities and approaches.

If you understand your customers' needs and think about the experience they have when making a purchase, you will be able to identify where you add value and where you will need to refine parts of the CBP as you grow. This can help you to convert leads – who may be interested in buying your product or service – into actual customers. You can find out more about this conversion process in one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Sales and Marketing, as you have the opportunity to visualize it using a Sales Funnel.

There are four main stages of the CBP:

### 1. Pre-purchase
In this stage, the customer completes the following steps to identify the purchase they wish to make:

**i. Identifies a need**
The customer has to recognize a problem they need to solve, or their desire for a product or service. This may be automatic or specific. For example, realizing that they have run out of a basic supply of cleaning products would be automatic, whilst deciding to organize a celebration for a family member would be specific.

**ii. Gathers information**
The customer then gathers information about the options available to them. For example, they may search online or ask friends for recommendations. In business-to-business (B2B) markets, the customer may send out a request for proposals from potential providers.

**iii. Evaluates the possibilities**
The customer then evaluates the information according to their problem or need, and personal priorities. During this stage the customer compares factors like price, quality, features, and convenience. They will also weigh the benefits of each option against the potential costs of switching from their current solution, such as time, money, or effort required. The marketing mix (product, price, place, and promotion) plays a key role here, as effective promotions, competitive pricing, and accessibility can sway the customer's decision.

This is why the majority of marketing and sales activities are focused on the pre-purchase stage of the CBP. However, you should also pay close attention to the other stages to ensure the customer completes the purchase and is satisfied with your product or service.

### 2. Purchase
It is possible to lose customers at this stage if the purchase process is complicated or causes problems for the customer.

During the purchase phase, it is important to consider the following:
- How you could simplify the payment process for your customers.
- How technology could make it easier to purchase your product or service (for example, an accessible online store).
- The payment terms offered to customers, particularly for B2B offerings.

### 3. Delivery and use
This stage can look very different depending on your business's particular offering. Consider the following:
- Is your delivery process simple and predictable?
- If you provide services related to delivery (such as installation), how efficient are your associated processes and documentation?
- How can you ensure a positive customer experience when the purchase and delivery phases are simultaneous (for example, in restaurants)?
- For service offerings with long contracts, the delivery and use stage may be long and involve multiple points of contact with the customer. How can you make the best use of these to ensure satisfaction and deliver value?

### 4. Post-purchase evaluation
The customer now has the opportunity to re-evaluate their choice if they are not satisfied with the product or service, or if they are unhappy with the customer experience they have received.

If the customer is happy with their decision, they can benefit your business by:
- becoming a repeat customer
- posting an online review
- referring other customers to your business.

However, unhappy customers could harm your success by:
- returning the product or cancelling the service
- making no further purchases
- sharing poor experiences and discouraging others from purchasing the product or service.

You should engage with your customers following their purchase (for example, by sending follow-up surveys or emails) to ensure that their perceptions of your business remain positive.

Evaluate the experiences of your target customer segments in the CBP. You will then be able to identify stages that offer clear value to your customers, and stages which require further development as you grow.

## 9. Maximizing Value in the Customer Buying Process (Video)

It has never been easier for customers to choose the product or service that best meets their needs, and to switch to a competitor if they are not satisfied. As a business owner, this responsiveness can seem overwhelming, but each stage of the Customer Buying Process offers you a valuable opportunity to stand out from your competition and make a lasting positive impression on your target customers. Even small changes can make a big impact on a customer's perception of you and your product or service. For example, after analyzing the ideal buying process for your target customers, you may choose to improve their customer experience through a streamlined online checkout or by offering a range of convenient delivery options.

### Example 1: Alice (Cake Business)
Alice analyzed her cake business and realized that most bakeries require orders two days in advance. She broke down her process: preparation takes 15 minutes, baking takes 15 minutes, and cooling is 30 minutes. By mobilizing her resources to prepare sponge cakes in advance, she created a walk-in service where customers can select a cake and toppings and watch as they mix. By offering a walk-in service, Alice adds value to customers in the purchase stage of the buying process. By doing this, she meets the needs of a category of people who would otherwise have gone elsewhere to buy a celebration cake.

### Example 2: Varsha (Jus Blouses)
Jus Blouses is a brand for retail as well as customizations. Finding ready-made blouses which people could just pick up and go was very hard. The offering was unique because there was no hassle of going to a tailor and getting things made. It's a one-stop shop for everything that a woman wants to wear – Western, Indian, bridal, occasion wear, formal, informal – anything can be done. You can customize anything made-to-order. Since customers are happy with the service, craftsmanship, and timely deliveries, they're ready to come back and place more orders. By offering customization of her garments within her retail stores, Varsha provides a unique and convenient buying process, providing everything her customers need in one place. Customers can tailor their garments to their specific requirements with greater creativity and control. Being able to create bespoke occasional wear all in one place is a unique market offering, which results in excellent customer satisfaction and repeat purchases.

Remember, each business owner adds meaningful value for customers in a way that is unique to their business. How will you give your own customers something extra and stand apart from the competition?

## 14. Finding More Information on Your Competitors (Reading)

Although information is easier to access than ever before, finding the data you need is not always straightforward. This can be particularly true when you are researching your competitors.

For example, if you wanted to find out more information about your competitors' internal operational processes and future strategy, where would you look? Some of the information may be available on their websites, but a lot will be confidential and not shared with the public.

## 15. Differentiating Yourself from Your Competitors (Reading)

Now that you have analyzed your competition, you can begin to define how you are different from your competition.

The unique value that you can offer customers is known as your competitive advantage. Differentiating yourself from competitors is a success factor in any competitive market and can support business growth.

A business can gain competitive advantage in three main ways, as follows:

### Cost advantage
The business delivers the same benefits as competitors, but at a lower cost.

### Differentiation advantage
The business delivers benefits that are better or more appealing than those offered by competitors.

### Focus advantage
The business has an in-depth understanding of its target customers, and produces specific offerings for a niche market.

You can develop your business's competitive advantage by carefully considering what your target customers value the most and adjusting your offer to better meet their needs. It is also important to consider how the external representation – or brand – of your business helps to convey these values. You can explore this further in one of the other 10,000 Women courses, Fundamentals of Sales and Marketing.

In this lesson, you will reflect on the following points to identify your business's current competitive advantage, and how you compare to the competition:
- Your target customer's main purchase criteria (examples include quality, price, and convenience of delivery).
- How you and your competitors perform against the criteria you have identified.
- Your areas of strength and those to be developed, depending on how important each factor is to your target customers.

Once you have done this, you will be able to consider how to communicate a clear message about your offering that differentiates you from the competition.

## 19. The Customer Value Proposition (Reading)

To attract and retain customers, it is not enough to offer them value – you need to communicate this value in a compelling way. The Customer Value Proposition (CVP) is a clear statement that will help focus internal and external communication about your product or service, and how your business meets the needs of its target customers.

You may have heard of another important statement that needs to be communicated to your customers: a Unique Selling Proposition (USP). CVP focuses on the overall value of products or services for the customer, by solving problems or fulfilling their needs. Your USP emphasizes what makes your product or service distinctive or even superior to those offered by competitors. In other words, CVP reflects why customers should choose you, while USP highlights what sets you apart from competition.

In this lesson, you will explore what makes a good Customer Value Proposition, review a range of Customer Value Propositions from other businesses, and begin to develop your own statement.

## 20. Introducing the Customer Value Proposition (Video)

Clearly communicating the value that you offer to your target customers is an important ongoing task for business owners. One key tool to help you do this effectively is the customer value proposition, a CVP. This is a statement which positions your business in the marketplace and makes explicit the value that you have to offer. Customer value propositions are not only useful to potential customers, but can also provide the basis for your marketing and branding activities. A clear articulation of your value helps to ensure everyone in your organization is actively engaged in delivering that value for your customers, improving their satisfaction and retention. The core purpose of your customer value statement is to communicate who your target customer is, what product or service you are offering them, how your offering meets their core needs, why your offering is unique in the marketplace, and what your relative price point is.

### Example: The Garden Plant Company

This CVP is for the Garden Plant Company who sells a range of garden plants to their customers, residential gardeners, and commercial landscapers. The CVP makes it clear that these plants are strong and healthy. Clear benefits for the customer while emphasizing the friendly and knowledgeable atmosphere, all of which sets the nursery apart from its competition. Finally, the CVP explicitly mentions that prices at the nursery are affordable and competitive, appealing to customers who want good value for money, as well as excellent service and quality.

It is important to create a clear customer value proposition as you develop your growth opportunity. It will become a key resource to help focus your business on addressing your core customer needs.

## 21. Understanding the Difference Between B2B and B2C CVPs (Reading)

Although it is important for all businesses to have a clear Customer Value Proposition, there are key differences between the statements used for business-to-consumer (B2C) and business-to-business (B2B) offerings. These variations are due to the different buying processes that businesses and consumers go through when selecting the product or service that is right for them.

### B2C Customer Value Propositions
Emotive language commonly appears in Customer Value Propositions for B2C products and services. The language used helps appeal to customers' desires, emotionally connecting them to your company and its product or service.

The B2C decision-making process is likely to involve one or two people, and is often guided by a combination of rational and emotional evaluation.

Customers are often encouraged to purchase a product or service because it will help them to:
- stay healthy
- live longer
- be happier
- pursue their passions.

Think of the brands you use or interact with on a daily basis. How many of them include emotional hooks in their value propositions?

### B2B Customer Value Propositions
B2B Customer Value Propositions, on the other hand, tend to focus on value drivers. They make it clear how the product or service offered will help the business to:
- save time
- reduce costs
- reduce risks
- improve quality or variety
- increase revenue
- increase operational efficiency.

Most businesses have a defined process for making purchases. This often involves a range of stakeholders from different departments, each with their own objective criteria for the purchase (such as price, or speed of delivery).

The evaluation process for business purchases is often very detailed, as all purchases can be subject to later examination and must be justified. Because of this, it is important to clearly demonstrate the measurable value that can be offered by your B2B product or service within your CVP.

Take some time to consider appropriate emotive language or value drivers that you could include when you write a CVP for your own business.

## 24. Your CVP and Pricing Strategy (Reading)

Your Customer Value Proposition (CVP) and pricing strategy are closely connected. Your CVP will direct and affect your decisions on pricing your product or service. Your price will also affect how your customers assess the value of your product or service. The ultimate realization of that value is in the customer making a purchase, and the price they are willing to pay.

Increasing prices is one of the easiest ways to increase revenue and profitability, but your prices will still need to meet the needs of your customers.

In this lesson, you will consider how much your target customers will be willing to pay for the value you offer. You will also evaluate strategies to maximize your profits.

Pricing is likely to impact many elements of your business, including the following:

### 1. Sales volumes
As you increase the price of your offering, your sales volumes may start falling. However, the higher prices will increase your profit margin on each sale, so your overall profit may rise. You will need to evaluate at what price and volume combination you maximize your overall profits.

Similarly, as you decrease the price, your sales volumes may go up, but your profit margins will come down. Again, you will need to find the price that enables you to maximize your overall profits.

### 2. Profitability
Increasing your prices is one of the most effective ways to increase your net profits. Many business owners try to improve profits by focusing on increasing the volume of sales, or reducing overheads, but pricing often has a far greater impact.

Price works so well as a lever because other costs do not increase alongside it. Raising volume also increases the cost of goods sold, but simply putting up the price does not do this.

A small increase in price could therefore lead to an increase in profitability, even if sales volumes are reduced.

### 3. Brand position
Pricing can have a direct impact on your brand image because customers tend to form assumptions about businesses based on their price point. It can help position you as a premium brand (if your pricing is comparatively expensive), or as a mass market brand (if your pricing is competitive). You will have to think carefully about what your target customers expect and whether they are willing to pay for your product or service.

## 25. Selecting a Pricing Model (Video)

Choosing the best pricing model is a key decision for any business owner. Small changes in how you price your product or service can have a significant impact on your business's profitability. It takes considerably less effort to raise your prices by five percent than to increase sales or reduce costs by that same amount. Identifying the right pricing strategy for your business is an important contributing factor to your ability to attract and retain customers. To do this confidently, you need to have a good understanding of your business opportunity, the market, and your target customers. You also need to explore the range of pricing options available to you.

There are three key pricing models that businesses typically adopt: reference-based pricing, cost-plus pricing, and value-based pricing.

As we consider each one in detail, think about which is the closest match to your current pricing strategy, whether there is scope to increase your prices since your last review, and the effects that changing your pricing strategy might have on your business as it grows.

### Reference-based pricing
Reference-based pricing is when you use the standard market pricing for a product or service as a reference for defining your own price. In other words, you look at the price charged by your competitors and then decide your price based on that. This approach can help you to remain competitive and comparable in the market. However, this pricing strategy raises key questions for a business owner to consider: Does a referenced-based approach take into consideration the unique value that you offer your target customers? Are your competitors the best benchmark for pricing in this context?

### Cost-plus pricing
Cost-plus pricing is when you set a price by calculating your costs and making the product or delivering the service, and add a set markup to generate a profit. It is important that all costs, fixed and variable, are included. This is a relatively simple model to use as business owners often tend to know their costs but may be less familiar with customer demand and the perceived value of their offering. However, this pricing strategy can lead to you undervaluing your product or service when the right target customer would be willing to pay a higher price.

### Value-based pricing
Value-based pricing involves calculating the perceived value your target customer places on your product or service in comparison to a competitor's offering and using this to define a price. Although it can be challenging to put a figure on the differentiated value you offer, this pricing model can be very effective if you focus on customer needs. If this is calculated correctly, this model can produce a win-win situation for you and your customers. This strategy can sometimes be challenging for business owners as it is difficult to get the price exactly right on the first try and important to respond to market demand quickly if you need to make adjustments.

This kind of swift response is much easier in some businesses than others. It is important to make sure that the pricing model you select is a good fit for your business and your customers. It may also be the case that your business offers multiple products or services. It can be very helpful to consider whether the same strategy is the most appropriate for each of these, or whether in fact the needs of your different customer types would be better met by adopting more than one pricing strategy within the wider business.

Remember, selecting the right pricing strategy for your business can have a significant positive impact on profitability. By applying your in-depth knowledge as a business owner and evaluating your business context carefully, you will find the best fit to support successful and sustainable growth.

## 26. Exploring Innovative Pricing Approaches (Reading)

Alongside the pricing models discussed in the video, many businesses use the following innovative pricing approaches in different ways. Consider your Customer Value Proposition (CVP) as you read through each one. Does your CVP support any of these approaches?

### Portfolio pricing
This pricing strategy involves looking at your offering as a whole, pricing some items low to attract customers, and generating greater profits on other items.

For example, a coffee shop may sell drinks at a low price (with low profit margins) to attract customers, but sell premium baked goods with a high profit margin.

### Bundle pricing
Bundle pricing encourages customers to purchase more products or services than they would otherwise consider by giving a discount when multiple items are purchased at the same time.

For example, a cable television service may offer access to each channel individually for a set price and a bundle of related channels at less than the total cost of each individually.

### Variable pricing
Sometimes, businesses use variable pricing strategies to increase sales during off-peak times or increase profits when demand is high.

For example, airlines may charge more to travel to popular holiday destinations during the summer months, while restaurants often offer deals early in the evening, to attract more customers during their quieter hours.

### Customized pricing
If your customers value choice and flexibility highly, you may want to consider allowing them to customize their pricing based on their exact needs.

This approach is often used by cell phone providers, who offer a range of options to meet the needs of a wide variety of customers. It is also commonly used in service sectors, where a requirement or brief is priced based on a specification from the customer.

### Freemium pricing
Freemium pricing allows a potential customer to use a product or service for free, but charges a premium for additional features or services.

This approach is commonly used in the software industry, but can also be applied to other business types. For example, a professional services firm might offer an hour's free consultation, but if documents are to be prepared then they may charge a fee.

### Anything as a Service (XaaS) pricing
A common pricing model used in the software industry is Software as a Service (SaaS), in which software is licensed on a usage or subscription basis.

This innovation has now grown beyond software. For example, there are a range of successful international businesses that provide media streaming for their subscribers (Entertainment as a Service). Many large cities also offer on-demand bike services (Transportation as a Service), where you can pay a monthly fee and hire bikes from anywhere in the city, then drop them off at a different location. This pricing model is known as Anything (X) as a Service (XaaS).

Now that you have reviewed a range of possible pricing approaches, you are ready to evaluate your current pricing strategy, and consider how this could be adapted to support ongoing business growth and increased profitability. You may want to investigate one or more of these pricing innovations in more detail before you complete your evaluation.
