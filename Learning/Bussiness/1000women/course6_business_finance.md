### Understanding key financial terms (Video)

Transcript:

The language of finance can be confusing, as there are often conflicting terms for the same concept, and regional variations in words and phrases. Let's take a simple example, and look at why understanding these terms can make such a positive difference to your business.

Judgments about the strength of a business often refer to it having a "strong" or "weak" Balance Sheet. This often relates to the value and type of assets the business owns and controls. Understanding what assets your business has, and how these can be categorized, can help you ensure that you are seen as a strong business.

An asset is anything owned or controlled by the business with a measurable economic value, for example, desks, machinery, inventory, and cash in the bank. Although you may say your staff are an asset to your business, this does not meet the definition of a financial asset, which is why you do not see them on a Balance Sheet.

Assets are split into fixed assets and current assets. Anything you expect to last for more than one year is a fixed asset – for example, buildings and machinery. Current assets, on the other hand, are things that you expect to change more quickly: cash, inventory, and debtors, also known as accounts receivable.

For example, debtors owe you money, and they are a current asset rather than a fixed asset, as you would hope you would not have to wait more than 12 months for them to pay.

Fixed assets are more varied than current assets, and are described as either tangible or intangible. Tangible assets are physical things that you could touch, like computers or vans. Intangible assets are things like intellectual property and patents.

However, as a business owner – how is this relevant to you? As the owner of a growing business, you have choices to make about where you invest your resources. The types of assets you acquire can influence the growth funding and rates that are available to you. Your assets can also influence whether a new supplier will deal with you, and what credit terms they may offer. Understanding financial terms helps you make the right decisions to enable your growth. Being able to speak the language of finance enables you to have better conversations with your financial advisors and team, and gain better support from them.

### Why is an Income Statement important? (Reading)

Let's take a look at an example of when a business owner might use an Income Statement.

Example: Alicia's business offers translation services in Mexico. In order to grow, she needs to expand one of two areas of her business: bulk translation or specialist technical language support.

How can an understanding of her finances help Alicia decide which area she should expand?

Ideally, Alicia's decision should be informed by understanding how profitable each area is. To determine this, she needs to know not only how much revenue is coming in, but also the costs incurred.

To be successful, she needs to understand the difference between the production costs and the overheads in her business. She also needs to be able to successfully match the revenues and the costs within the right time periods.

The foundation for all this analysis, and for good decision-making, can be found in her Income Statement.

Other uses for Income Statements:
- Imagine you began to operate in a new location last year, as part of your business expansion. How would you know whether that location has been profitable and should be continued?
- One of your designers has come up with a new product or service. How would you know whether it would be profitable to launch it?
- The space next to your factory will shortly become vacant and you are considering renting it. How would you assess whether this is a sensible business decision?

### What is an Income Statement? (Video)

Transcript:

An Income Statement shows your business's revenues and expenses over a given period of time, such as a month, quarter, or year. You use it to accurately calculate how much profit or loss your business has made in that period. It is also referred to as the Profit and Loss Statement, P&L, or the Statement of Revenue and Expense.

At the top of the statement is the revenue section, covering different types of income. The next section looks at expenses widely known as direct costs, which in your statement may be called cost of goods sold, or other terms. These are costs that increase proportionally with increased sales, rather than general overheads.

For example, in a manufacturing business, this would include the costs of inventory, production, and production labor.

Gross profit is the difference between the revenue earned and the direct costs, or cost of goods sold. For example, if you sell $1,000 of goods and they cost you $250 to produce, then your gross profit is $750. Gross profit is a useful measure to compare the financial effectiveness of your key processes over time, and to help you see how appropriate your costs and pricing are.

Indirect costs are often recorded as selling, general and administrative expenses, or operating costs. These are costs that do not directly increase or decrease according to the amount of sales a business makes. These are the costs to keep the business running, for example, marketing, rent, wages, and utilities.

When you deduct indirect costs from gross profit, you get a more comprehensive profit value called operating profit, or operating income, that tells you more about how much money your business is actually making.

This value is the basis of a common calculation called EBITDA - Earnings Before Interest, Taxation, Depreciation and Amortization. EBITDA can be used to compare businesses against each other, even if they are of different types or sizes.

EBITDA is the most common measure used by external funding providers to calculate the profitability of the business. It also forms the basis of business valuations so it is an incredibly important metric to manage.

The rest of the statement looks at the effects of tax, financing, and accounting decisions resulting in your net income.

### Recording information on Income Statements (Reading)

As you have seen, in theory, Income Statements are a simple list of revenues and costs over a defined time period. However, in practice, business operations often do not align perfectly with the specific time periods covered by an Income Statement.

In business, certain things like inventory and credit transactions often happen over multiple reporting periods. To make sure everything adds up correctly, there are specific accounting methods used to track them.

Inventory: Income Statements do not show unsold inventory for the period. For example, in one month, a trading business has sourced 100 units of product and sold 80 units. On its Income Statement, it records revenue received from the sale of the 80 units. For the costs, it declares the price paid for 80 units, not 100 units. The costs for the remaining 20 units will be declared on the next month's Income Statement when they are sold. This approach is called the matching concept.

Credit: Many businesses offer credit to customers, where the customer receives goods or services, and promises to make a delayed payment. In this case, the matching concept requires the revenue to be recorded on the Income Statement when the delivery is made, not when the payment is received from customers.

Similarly, businesses often receive credit from their suppliers. In this case, the matching concept requires the associated costs to be recorded on the Income Statement when the goods or services are received, rather than when you pay your supplier.

Depreciation: Businesses often acquire expensive assets, such as machinery, which will be useful for several years. Charging the full cost of an asset to the Income Statement in the period it is purchased would distort the financial figures. To provide a more accurate representation, its cost is allocated over its useful life through a process called depreciation.

Example - Maria's shoe business: Maria runs a small shoe business, selling high-quality handmade shoes. She sources her shoes from a local supplier at $25 each, paying upfront in cash. This week, she bought five pairs of shoes, costing her $125 in total. Maria sells one pair to her neighbour Emma for $60 cash. Another customer, Joanna, takes a pair but says she will pay Maria $60 next week.

Profit Calculation:
- Revenue: Sale to Emma ($60) + Sale to Joanna on credit ($60) = $120
- Cost of Goods Sold: 2 x $25 = $50
- Profit: $120 - $50 = $70

Cash Position Calculation:
- Cash inflow: Sale to Emma = $60
- Cash outflow: Payment for shoes (5 x $25) = $125
- Net Cash Position: $60 - $125 = -$65

This example demonstrates that effective cash flow management is essential. A profitable business can still face financial difficulties because the timing of cash inflows and outflows does not always align with the figures on the Income Statement.

### Why is a Balance Sheet important? (Reading)

Let's look at an example of when a business owner might use a Balance Sheet.

Example: Malee owns a manufacturing unit that produces paper-based packaging material for small businesses in Thailand. She is switching to a more environmentally friendly adhesive, so is negotiating with a new supplier.

The supplier is considering whether or not to provide her with credit, and what terms to offer her. The supplier needs to know how healthy Malee's business is to assess whether they should trust her to make future payments. A useful source of information for the supplier is Malee's Balance Sheet, which allows them to compare her assets and liabilities (debts), and to gauge the stability of her business.

Other uses for a Balance Sheet:
- Do you have enough assets in your business to enable you to plan long-term growth?
- You are pleased to be growing your revenues but are concerned that your inventory is getting out of control – how can you find out if this is true?
- Are you maintaining a healthy balance between your assets and liabilities?
- Are your debt levels manageable or is your business taking on too much?
- Do you have the financial flexibility to invest in new opportunities?

### What is a Balance Sheet? (Video)

Transcript:

A Balance Sheet shows how all the business's finances (including investments, debts, and sales) are connected. It allows you to see the overall financial health of your business, but is only a snapshot at a particular moment in time. As your Balance Sheet is often what the financial community can see for a whole year, it is important to understand and manage this annual 'financial photograph'.

It has three major sections: assets, liabilities, and owners' equity. You can think of it as showing what assets the business owns and how they have been funded, either through the shareholders' money or through external funding.

As discussed previously, your assets are the resources that are owned or controlled by the business.

A liability is a debt owed to someone outside the business. Liabilities are not a bad thing and many high growth businesses make good use of external debt funding and supplier growth. As you will see later, the key thing is to ensure the proportion of liabilities to assets is well managed.

As with assets, liabilities are divided into short- and long-term. Current liabilities are debts the business needs to pay off in the next year, usually operational expenses such as payments to suppliers. Long-term liabilities or debts, are payment obligations the business needs to meet in the next few years, such as long-term loans and mortgages.

Business owners often reinvest most of their profits into their business, to ensure that there is money readily available.

Owners' equity, also known as capital and reserves, is a combination of the capital invested by the owners and the running total of the profits made by the business, but not paid out to the shareholders.

Your Balance Sheet may not be as familiar to you as your Income Statement, but it is used as much, if not more, by the financial community to judge your business. As a business owner, you should find it just as useful. Rapid growth can be risky, so you need to know that your business is financially healthy, and the Balance Sheet is a key tool to help you assess this.

### Using financial ratios (Video)

Transcript:

Financial statements give you vital information about your business. The individual numbers, however, may not always give you a full picture of how well the business is doing, or help you make decisions. You can find out more by combining different values to form ratios, which offer key insights into your business's operations and health.

Profitability ratios describe the business's ability to turn sales into profits and cash flow. The most common ratios in this category are gross profit margin, operating margin, and net profit margin, and they all use a similar calculation.

For example, Sarah's flower business has made $50,000 operating profit this year. This is clearly a significant profit, but on its own, this figure does not tell Sarah much about her business performance or growth. Looking back, this is $25,000 more in operating profit than last year – so you can see that there has been growth.

If you calculate the margin this year on her turnover, you see it is 10 percent. However, if you compare this to last year, you can see that though sales and profits have both increased, her profit growth has not been as fast as her revenue growth, raising questions about why her expenses are scaling faster than sales.

Liquidity ratios indicate the stability of your business operations. The most common liquidity ratio is the current ratio, which is the ratio of current assets to current liabilities. This ratio indicates your business's ability to pay its short-term bills, and compares your short-term assets to your short-term liabilities.

Sarah's current assets have increased along with her sales, but so have her current liabilities. Using the current ratio, we can see that her accounts payable – the money she owes to suppliers – is scaling more quickly than her assets. This may mean her business is less stable than last year, as well as having lower margins.

While liquidity ratios look at your business's short-term stability, solvency ratios indicate the long-term financial stability of your business. They measure all of your business's debt against all of its assets. If your debt burden is very high, you may not have the flexibility to manage your cash flow if business slows down or interest rates rise.

Sarah has taken on a long-term loan in her business that has increased her total liabilities. She has also increased her fixed assets with a new shop, but what is the net effect? The solvency ratio lets you compare the new situation with the past.
