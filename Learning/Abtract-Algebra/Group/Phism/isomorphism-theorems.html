What Are Isomorphism Theorems?

Isomorphism theorems reveal deep connections between different ways of constructing groups. They show that seemingly different constructions actually produce the same structure.

🎯 Key Insight

Quotient groups and homomorphic images are the same thing! When you collapse a group by a normal subgroup, you get the same structure as the image of a homomorphism.

First Isomorphism Theorem

First Isomorphism Theorem
If φ: G → H is a group homomorphism, then:
G / ker(φ) ≅ im(φ)

The quotient of G by the kernel is isomorphic to the image of φ

What Does This Mean?

Starting Point: G
Quotient: G / ker(φ)
Image: im(φ)

Step-by-Step Proof Visualization

Building the Isomorphism

Step 1: Define the Map
Define ψ: G/ker(φ) → im(φ) by ψ(g·ker(φ)) = φ(g)

We're creating a map from cosets to image elements

Step 2: Well-Defined
If g₁K = g₂K, then g₁⁻¹g₂ ∈ K, so φ(g₁⁻¹g₂) = e', thus φ(g₁) = φ(g₂)

The map doesn't depend on which coset representative we choose

Step 3: Homomorphism
ψ((g₁K)(g₂K)) = ψ(g₁g₂K) = φ(g₁g₂) = φ(g₁)φ(g₂) = ψ(g₁K)ψ(g₂K)

The map preserves the group operation

Step 4: Bijective
Surjective: every φ(g) comes from gK. Injective: ψ(gK) = e' ⇒ φ(g) = e' ⇒ g ∈ K ⇒ gK = K

The map is one-to-one and onto, hence an isomorphism

Collapsing G by the kernel gives you exactly the image! The quotient construction and the homomorphic image are structurally identical.

Second Isomorphism Theorem

Second Isomorphism Theorem
If H ≤ G and N ⊴ G, then:
(HN) / N ≅ H / (H ∩ N)

The product modulo N is isomorphic to H modulo the intersection

Understanding the Setup

📋 Notation
  • H ≤ G means H is a subgroup of G
  • N ⊴ G means N is a normal subgroup of G
  • HN = {hn : h ∈ H, n ∈ N} is the product subgroup
  • H ∩ N is the intersection (also a subgroup)

Step-by-Step Proof

Proof Strategy
1
2
3
Step 1: Define the Homomorphism
Define φ: H → (HN)/N by φ(h) = hN

Map each element of H to its coset in the quotient

Step 2: Find the Kernel
ker(φ) = {h ∈ H : hN = N} = {h ∈ H : h ∈ N} = H ∩ N

The kernel is exactly the intersection!

Step 3: Apply First Theorem
By First Isomorphism Theorem: H/ker(φ) ≅ im(φ), so H/(H ∩ N) ≅ (HN)/N

The second theorem follows from the first!

Comparing the Theorems

First Isomorphism Theorem
G/ker(φ) ≅ im(φ)

Purpose

Relates quotient groups to homomorphic images

Key Insight

Quotienting by the kernel gives the image

Use When

You have a homomorphism and want to understand its structure

Second Isomorphism Theorem
(HN)/N ≅ H/(H∩N)

Purpose

Relates product quotients to intersection quotients

Key Insight

Two ways of collapsing give the same result

Use When

You have a subgroup and normal subgroup interacting

The Connection

🔗 Beautiful Relationship

The Second Theorem is proved using the First! This shows the First Isomorphism Theorem is the fundamental result—the Second is a clever application.
